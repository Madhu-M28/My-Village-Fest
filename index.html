About the Festival

India is known as the land of festivals, and diversity is its identity. People of different castes, religions and communities live here, and all the people have their own festivals and different way of celebrating them. Different states and regions have their own regional festivals. Among such festivals, there is one unique festival that is gaining the attention of the masses, especially cow lovers, which is the Gorehabba Cow Dung Festival. We will deeply explore this festival, its origin, and its cultural view.

In a remote village GUMATAPURAM in Thalavadi in Tamil Nadu’s Erode district, the end of Diwali is celebrated in, what many would call, a bizarre manner. But, it’s a celebration that is about 300 years old where villagers gather to mark the end of Diwali festival.

Our village festival is a joyful occasion that brings everyone together to celebrate traditions, food, music, and culture. Every year, families and friends gather to share stories, dance, and enjoy delicious meals. This website will share festival highlights, photos, and updates.

As Diwali comes to an end, residents of Gumatapura village situated about 180 kms south of Bengaluru in Karnataka, celebrated the cow dung festival. Joyful crowds pelted each other with fistfuls of cow manure of our village's local ritual to mark the end of Diwali, India's most important Hindu festival.

Opening Ceremony

The Gorehabba festival begins with the collection of dung from cow-owning homes in the Gumatapura village, which is located on the border of Karnataka and Tamil Nadu. The 'Gorehabba' festival is an annual festival, which was celebrated even during the COVID-19 outbreak. During the festival, people fling and smear one another with cow dung. The festival is celebrated to mark an end to Diwali celebrations. At first, the tractor trolleys transport the dung to the local temple, where a priest conducts the blessing ceremony. The excrement is then deposited in an open location, where men and boys wade through to smear each other with cow dung. People from all over the nation visit Gumatapuram each year to witness the cow dung conflict.

Our festival opened with a colorful parade showcasing traditional dances, costumes, and music.

The Gorehabba festival begins with the afternoon collection of "ammunition" from cow-owning homes in the village.

Then it is piled onto tractors pulled by cattle adorned with marigold flowers, and brought to a local temple where priests perform a blessing ritual. The blessed cow dung is dumped in an open area of Gumatapura, and topless daredevils rush to it to prepare their projectiles. The battle is a fierce one, with bits of manure flying all over the place, and if you are brave enough to stick around to film the event, be careful as you risk getting hit by stray shrapnel!

@ 8:00 AM

Festivals in villages are always filled with joy, tradition, and togetherness. They bring the entire community under one roof, where every person, from the youngest child to the oldest elder, takes part in the celebrations. One such vibrant festival took place in our village, and it remains one of the most memorable experiences for everyone. The day started early in the morning, when all the villagers gathered at the temple, which is considered the heart of our community. The children, also assembled at the temple to seek the blessings of the deity. The temple was beautifully decorated with flowers, mango leaves, and colorful rangoli at the entrance. The fragrance of incense sticks filled the air, and devotional songs created a spiritual atmosphere. After performing the rituals, the priest blessed the children, and everyone felt a deep sense of happiness and devotion. After receiving blessings, the children came out of the temple, filled with excitement and energy. Their laughter and playful spirit added a lively touch to the festival mood. They began to play near the temple and then moved through different streets of the village. The tradition of children playing during the festival is very special, as it symbolizes joy, innocence, and unity.

Benefits of the Gorehabba Festival 1. Community Bonding Brings villagers together in a joyful, playful way. Strengthens relationships among neighbors, families, and different age groups. 2. Cultural Preservation Keeps alive a unique village tradition that has been practiced for generations. Helps younger generations understand and value their heritage. 3. Spiritual & Symbolic Value Cow dung is considered sacred and purifying in Hindu tradition. Playing with it is believed to remove negativity and bring blessings of health, prosperity, and fertility. 4. Stress Relief & Joy The playful throwing of cow dung acts like a “festival of fun” (similar to Holi with colors). Provides laughter, relaxation, and emotional release for everyone. 5. Agricultural Importance Highlights the value of cows and cow dung in rural life (used as fertilizer, fuel, disinfectant, and building material). Reminds people of their connection with nature and farming. 6. Tourism & Recognition The uniqueness of the festival attracts media, tourists, and researchers. Puts the village on the map, spreading awareness of India’s cultural diversity. 7. Symbol of Equality During the festival, all villagers—rich or poor, young or old—take part equally. This helps break down social barriers and promotes unity.

@ 9:00AM TO 12PM

A bullock cart is a traditional two-wheeled or four-wheeled cart pulled by cows, mainly used in villages for transporting goods, crops, or even people but here we are using to transport cow dung.

For the young men taking part in Gorehabba, hurling and getting hit with balls of cow dung is both about fun and a belief in the benefits of manure. Many believe that simply touching the blessed dung with your hands, you can be cured of all diseases, and you also have a big chance of never getting sick. In any case, if you’re wondering how the Gorehabba cow dung battle began, apparently locals believe that one of their deities,Beereshwara Swamy was born in cow dung. And, Interestingly, this is one of the famous cow dung battle in India. also engage in an epic cow dung cake battle which count dozens injured every year. They believe the tradition brings them good health and prosperity.

The manure is brought to the local temple on tractor trolleys, before a priest performs a blessing ritual.After that, the dung is dumped in an open area -- with men and boys wading in to prepare their weapons for the battle ahead. People flock to Gumatapura from far-flung cities each year, and for those in attendance, the messy battle is as much about fun as it is about the perceived health benefits.

@ 12:00PM TO 1:00PM

The children have completed their playing And they came out of the village, then went to Kumbeswarar Temple where there used to be a debate among themselves.

At the pond, the youths performed puja for the donkey. Then two of them sat on the donkey and roamed around the village. After that, they stopped in the temple and started playing.

@4:00PM TO 6:30PM

After finishing their play, they end the games at 5:00 pm and go to a place called Konda Karana Gudda, which is located at the end of the village. There, they perform some puja, burn an effigy (a statue of someone), and also sacrifice and burn a hen. This marks the conclusion of the festival. After that, they return to the ponds, take a bath, wear new clothes, and then go to the temple instead of going home. At the temple, they perform puja, receive blessings from the deity, and finally return home to enjoy the festival .
